So You Received an Inspection on Your New Homeowners Policy
A guide for Texas homeowners — and anyone shopping for coverage
Most homeowners assume that once they have insurance, the hard part is over. But a property inspection can upend your coverage, cancel your policy, or quietly raise your premiums — all without much warning. Whether you’re shopping for a new carrier or simply renewing, understanding how inspections work is one of the most important things you can do to protect your home and your budget.
Inspections are most common when you switch to a new carrier, but they can also happen at renewal — and when they do, the consequences can be just as significant. More on that in a moment.

Part One: The Hidden Risk of Switching Insurance Carriers
Shopping around for a better rate is smart financial planning, and switching carriers is something many homeowners do without any issues at all. That said, it’s worth understanding one aspect of the process that can catch people off guard: when you move to a new insurance carrier, that carrier has never seen your home. To protect themselves, most insurers order a physical property inspection within the first 30 to 60 days of a new policy — and what that inspector finds can matter.
Your previous carrier may have grandfathered in conditions they once accepted, or overlooked issues that accumulated over the years. A brand-new set of eyes — and a brand-new underwriting standard — changes that calculus entirely.
This is especially relevant in Texas, where the insurance market has tightened considerably following years of costly weather events. Carriers are scrutinizing new business more carefully than ever, and inspectors are under pressure to flag anything that represents a liability — even minor cosmetic issues that might have passed unnoticed a decade ago.
Just because your old carrier accepted your home’s condition doesn’t mean your new one will. Inspection standards vary significantly between insurance companies, and what was acceptable under one policy may be grounds for cancellation under another.
It’s also worth knowing what most inspections actually look like. The vast majority — well over 90% — are exterior-only. An inspector drives by or walks the perimeter, photographs the roof line, siding, yard, and visible structural elements, and moves on. They are not coming inside to open your cabinet doors or check your water heater. For most standard homes, this is a quick and relatively limited review.
High value homes are a different story. Carriers insuring properties above certain thresholds — typically $1,000,000 or more in replacement cost, though this varies by carrier — often require a more thorough interior inspection as part of the underwriting process. These reviews are more detailed, covering mechanical systems, interior finishes, and overall upkeep. High value home insurance is a topic that deserves its own discussion, and we’ll cover it in a separate article.
The advice? Before you switch, do a thorough walk-around of your property with honest eyes. Ask yourself what an inspector might flag from the street or the perimeter. Addressing issues proactively — before the inspection — can mean the difference between seamless coverage and a difficult conversation with your new carrier.

Part Two: Inspections Can Happen at Renewal Too
Most people are caught off guard when they learn that an inspection isn’t just a new-policy event. Carriers do occasionally inspect homes at renewal, particularly if there’s been a recent claim, if the property hasn’t been reviewed in several years, or if the carrier is conducting a broader audit of their book of business in a particular area or zip code.
Renewal inspections are less frequent than new-policy inspections, but they carry their own serious consequence: a non-renewal. Rather than canceling your policy mid-term, the carrier simply declines to offer you coverage again when your current term expires. You’ll receive a non-renewal notice — typically 30 to 60 days before your expiration date in Texas — and then you’re on your own to find replacement coverage before the deadline.
A non-renewal based on inspection findings is not the same as a cancellation, but the practical effect is similar. You lose your coverage, potentially in a hard market where finding a comparable replacement at a comparable price is difficult. And unlike a mid-term cancellation, there’s less urgency built into the timeline — which means some homeowners don’t take the notice seriously until it’s too late.
If you receive a non-renewal notice, treat it with the same urgency as a cancellation. Begin shopping immediately, address any flagged conditions as quickly as possible, and ask your agent whether remediation could prompt the carrier to reconsider.

Part Three: Common Conditions Inspectors Flag — and What They Cost You
Inspectors aren’t looking for perfection. They’re looking for conditions that represent elevated risk of a claim. Understanding what’s on their checklist helps you prepare — and potentially avoid costly surprises. These are the conditions that come up most frequently:
Debris in the yard. Accumulated wood piles, brush, old equipment, or junk signal neglect and create fire and liability hazards. Insurers see this as a maintenance failure, and it’s one of the easiest problems to fix before an inspector ever shows up.
Peeling or deteriorating paint. More than cosmetic, peeling exterior paint exposes wood to moisture, leading to rot and structural damage over time. Inspectors treat it as a deferred maintenance red flag that suggests broader neglect.
Missing or damaged shingles. A compromised roof is the single most common reason for inspection-related policy changes. Even a few missing shingles can indicate broader weathering or unrepaired hail damage, and most carriers will require documentation of repairs before binding or continuing coverage.
Trees overhanging the roof. Limbs hanging over a structure represent a significant wind and storm risk. Carriers may require trimming as a condition of coverage — at the homeowner’s expense, typically running $200 to $1,500 or sometimes thousands depending on the size and number of trees involved.
Deteriorating decks or fencing. Rotting wood structures create liability risks. An old deck with missing boards or a leaning fence can raise questions about general upkeep across the entire property, not just the structure in question.
Visible electrical or plumbing concerns. Exposed wiring, outdated panels, or water staining visible from the exterior may prompt requests for an interior inspection — or carrier withdrawal from the risk entirely.
A practical tip: walk your property the week before a new policy takes effect. Spend a few hours on basic cleanup — removing debris, trimming low-hanging branches, touching up peeling trim paint. These small investments can prevent a much larger headache.

Part Four: How an Inspection Can Trigger Cancellation in the First 60 Days
In Texas, insurance carriers are granted a window of flexibility at the start of a new policy. During the first 60 days — sometimes called the underwriting period — a carrier has the legal right to cancel a policy for almost any reason, provided they give adequate notice. This window exists so insurers can complete their due diligence before committing to long-term coverage.
After that initial period, Texas law significantly limits a carrier’s ability to cancel. But during those first 60 days, an inspection report with flagged conditions can give a carrier grounds to send a cancellation notice — sometimes with as little as 10 days of advance notice.
Here’s how that timeline typically plays out:
Day 1: Your policy binds and coverage takes effect. You’re technically insured — but the underwriting review process has already begun behind the scenes.
Days 15–45: Most carriers dispatch an inspector. The inspector photographs the exterior, roof, yard, and any visible structural features. You may or may not be home during this visit — it’s often done from the street and perimeter.
Days 30–60: The inspection report lands on an underwriter’s desk. If conditions are flagged, you may receive a letter requesting repairs within a set timeframe, or in more serious cases, a cancellation notice. Texas requires at least 10 days notice for cancellations during this period, which gives you some time to respond — but not much.
Day 61 and beyond: Your position becomes considerably stronger. Texas carriers face much stricter limitations and can generally only cancel for non-payment, fraud, or a substantial increase in hazard. Getting through that initial window is the goal.
Other states have similar underwriting windows, though the specifics vary. Many states allow 30 to 90 days of open cancellation rights for new policies. The general principle is the same everywhere: new policies carry far more cancellation risk than established ones. This is one more reason why switching carriers is not a decision to make lightly, or without first preparing your property.
If your policy is cancelled in the first 60 days and you haven’t yet secured a replacement, you may find yourself temporarily uninsured — a serious problem if you carry a mortgage, since lenders require continuous coverage. Always have a backup plan, and begin shopping for replacement coverage the moment you receive any cancellation notice.

Part Five: When an Inspection Increases Your Coverage — and Your Premium
Here’s a scenario most homeowners don’t anticipate: you switch carriers to save money, and the inspection results in a higher premium than you were originally quoted. How does this happen?
The answer lies in replacement cost valuations. When a new inspector visits your property, they may form a different opinion than your previous carrier about what it would cost to rebuild your home from scratch. This figure — called the dwelling coverage or Coverage A — is the foundation of your homeowners policy. Different inspectors use different methodologies, and those differences can have a real dollar impact on your bill.
Common reasons inspections lead to increased coverage amounts include upgrades not captured in a previous policy — a renovated kitchen, added square footage, a finished garage — as well as significant construction cost inflation that has pushed rebuild estimates higher across the board. The new carrier may also use a more conservative cost-per-square-foot model, or the inspector may identify higher-quality materials (custom cabinetry, engineered hardwood, slate roofing) that carry a higher replacement value than what was on record.
It’s worth stepping back and acknowledging that being underinsured is a genuine financial risk. If you carry $260,000 in dwelling coverage but it would actually cost $380,000 to rebuild your home after a total loss, you’d face a devastating shortfall. In that light, an inspection-driven increase in coverage — while it stings at the time — may actually be protecting you from a far worse outcome.
That said, you have options if the new estimate feels inflated. Ask your agent for a copy of the replacement cost estimator the carrier used. You have the right to review the methodology and the inputs. If you believe the figure is too high, an independent estimate can serve as a reasoned counterpoint. Documentation of your home’s actual materials, age of systems, and renovation history gives you something concrete to work with in that conversation. But at the end of the day, its up to the carrier if they want to lower the coverage no matter how good of a case you make.
Before switching carriers, ask your current agent to forward their replacement cost estimate. Make sure to discuss any discrepancies you feel are on there. This gives you a baseline to compare against, and reduces the likelihood of an unpleasant surprise after your new carrier’s inspection comes in higher than expected.

Protecting Yourself: What to Do Before, During, and After an Inspection
The good news is that property inspections can be very manageable when you know what to expect. A little preparation goes a long way toward keeping your coverage stable and your premiums grounded in reality.
•	Walk your property before switching carriers. Clear debris, trim overhanging branches, and address peeling paint or visibly damaged shingles. Small cosmetic fixes can prevent big underwriting problems.
•	Request a copy of your inspection report.  See what the inspector found, review it carefully, and respond promptly to any listed conditions before they escalate to a cancellation or non-renewal decision.
•	Never let coverage lapse. Whether you receive a mid-term cancellation or a non-renewal notice, begin shopping for replacement coverage immediately. Don’t wait until the final day — even a brief gap creates mortgage compliance issues and leaves your home exposed.
•	Understand your replacement cost number. Ask your agent to walk you through the Coverage A estimate and explain how it was calculated. Know what your policy is actually based on and whether it reflects your home’s current condition.
•	Work with an independent agent. An independent agent represents multiple carriers and can help you find one whose underwriting standards align with your home’s condition before you commit to a switch — saving you the disruption of a midstream cancellation or an unexpected non-renewal.
•	Document your improvements. Keep photos and receipts for any renovations or upgrades. If an inspector estimates too high, having documentation of actual materials and workmanship gives you a foundation for a reasoned appeal.
